## short article
Migration, immigration, and emigration all refer to moving, but from different perspectives: migration is the general, overarching process of moving from one place to another. Emigration (prefix E- for exit) is leaving one’s home country to live elsewhere. Immigration (prefix I- for in) is entering and settling in a new country. 

Key Differences and Contexts

Migration: A broad term for the movement of people, whether across international borders (international migration) or within a country (internal migration/in-migration/out-migration). It can be temporary or permanent.

Emigration (Exiting): Focuses on the point of departure. People emigrate from a country.

Immigration (Incoming): Focuses on the point of arrival. People immigrate to a country. 

Memory Aids & Examples

Emigrate = Exit your country.

Immigrate = Inter a new country.

Example: Someone might emigrate from Ireland to the United States, becoming an immigrant upon arrival. 

Common Motivations

Push Factors (Emigration): Reasons to leave, such as economic instability, conflict, or lack of opportunities.

Pull Factors (Immigration): Reasons to settle, such as better jobs, safety, or education.
