Whether you should include whitespace around a symbol depends on the symbol's function: specifically, whether it acts as a "connector" (joining two things) or a "modifier" (changing a single value). 
## 1. When to Use Whitespace
Use spaces when a symbol acts like a verb or conjunction connecting two separate parts. 

* Mathematical Operations: Place spaces on both sides of operators when they show a relationship between two numbers ($5 + 2 = 7$).
* Comparison Symbols: Use spaces around relations like greater than ($>$), less than ($<$), or equals ($=$).
* Measurement Units: Standard international guidelines (SI) generally require a space between a number and its unit (e.g., $10\ kg$, $5\ m$, $37\ °C$).
* Alternatives with Compound Words: While a slash (/) usually has no space, add spaces if one of the terms is a multi-word phrase (e.g., "high school / college level") to improve legibility. 

## 2. When NOT to Use Whitespace
Avoid spaces when a symbol is "attached" to a value as a modifier or adjective. 

* Negative/Positive Signs: Do not put a space between the sign and the number when indicating a value's position (e.g., $-10$, $+5$).
* Currency: Standard symbols like the dollar sign ($) or pound (£) are placed directly against the number (e.g., $\$100$).
* Percentages and Angles: In most general writing, do not use a space before the percent sign ($10\%$) or the degree symbol for angles ($45°$).
* Standard Slashes: Use no space when separating single words (e.g., "and/or", "yes/no").
* Inside Punctuation: There should be no space on the "inside" of quotation marks or parentheses. 

## Summary Table for Quick Reference

| Symbol Category  | Example | Spacing Rule |
|---|---|---|

| Comparison | $x < 20$ | Space on both sides |
